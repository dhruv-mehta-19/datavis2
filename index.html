The Heart of Coffee

How Melbourne's coffee world works — from bean to cup

By Dhruv Mehta · FIT2179 Data Visualisation 2 · May 2026

Melbourne isn't the city where Australians drink the most coffee — by national figures, other capitals rival or beat it. So why is Melbourne famous for it? Rather than claim Melbourne is "best," this is a look at how the city's coffee world actually works: where the beans come from, where they're drunk, and what ends up in your cup.

01The World That Supplies It

Before a bean lands in a Carlton café, it travels through Australia's national import network. In 2023, Australia imported unroasted coffee from over 50 countries — but a handful do almost all the work. Brazil alone supplies nearly twice the value of Colombia, our second-largest source.

Reading the map: a choropleth shades each country by import value. The dark band across Latin America shows where Australia's coffee overwhelmingly originates, while most of the world contributes little or nothing — making our supply geographically narrow.

Ranked by value, that concentration becomes stark. The largest suppliers carry a hugely disproportionate share of the total.

Reading the chart: the bars rank suppliers by import value; the line tracks the running cumulative share. Just three countries supply almost half of all imports, and the top ten cover roughly 85% — a concentrated, and therefore vulnerable, supply chain.

The same suppliers, ranked simply by value, confirm the pecking order at a glance.

Reading the chart: a horizontal bar ranks each country's total import value, making the gap between Brazil and everyone else immediately legible.

02Premium Beans vs Bulk Beans

Not every bean is equal. Vietnam supplies cheap robusta at the lowest price of any major partner; at the other extreme, tiny quantities of Jamaican Blue Mountain command many times that price. Australia imports both — the workhorses that fill chain-store espressos, and the luxury beans bound for specialty cafés.

Reading the chart: each bubble is a country, positioned by quantity (log scale) against price per kilogram, sized by total value. The spread from the cheap, high-volume bottom-right to the premium, low-volume top-left visualises the entire premium-versus-bulk trade in one view.

03The City That Drinks It

The City of Melbourne council alone hosts thousands of cafés and restaurants packed into a few square kilometres. The CBD dominates, but Carlton's Lygon Street has been a cultural heart of Melbourne's coffee scene since Italian and Greek migrants brought espresso here in the mid-20th century.

Reading the map: every café is a point, sized by total seats and coloured by size category, over the council's neighbourhood boundaries. Click the legend to filter. The dense dark cluster shows how venues concentrate in the CBD and along Lygon Street. (Data covers the City of Melbourne council only — St Kilda, Richmond and most of South Yarra fall under other councils.)

Counted another way, the imbalance is even clearer.

Reading the chart: a treemap sizes each neighbourhood by its number of venues. The CBD's single block dwarfs every other area combined — the spatial heart of the city's hospitality.

This concentration didn't appear overnight. It's the result of two decades of steady growth in the city's dining scene.

Reading the chart: an area chart tracks distinct cafés and restaurants from 2002 to 2024 — the hospitality scene Melbourne's coffee culture grew within. It climbs steadily to a 2019 peak, dips with the COVID-19 closures, then recovers. (This counts all cafés and restaurants, ANZSIC class 4511, not coffee venues specifically.)

04Your Cup: Its Rhythm and Its Price

When is the city actually out and about? Pedestrian sensors reveal a daily rhythm that maps closely onto when coffee is bought — a morning ritual more than an all-day habit.

Reading the chart: two lines compare average foot traffic by hour on weekdays versus weekends, during café trading hours (6am–3pm). Weekdays spike sharply with the 8am commute — coffee's busiest window — while weekends start slow and build to a midday brunch crowd. Foot traffic is a proxy for when demand peaks, not a direct count of coffee buyers.

So why does a flat white cost what it does? Most of your money isn't going to the beans or the milk.

Reading the chart: a stacked bar breaks a $5 cup into its costs on a busy day versus a quiet one. Because labour is paid per shift regardless of cups sold, a slow day doubles labour-per-cup and collapses the café's margin — showing how thin the economics are. (Cost breakdown from Fish River Roasters; quiet-day profit is the exact arithmetic figure, ≈$0.89 per the source.)

And that cup has been getting steadily more expensive.

Reading the chart: a line shows the percentage change in Melbourne coffee prices since 2017. Flat for years, prices climbed sharply from 2022 as global bean supply tightened — the same shortage visible at the origin end of this story now showing up in your cup. (ABS index for the "coffee, tea & cocoa" class; shows price change, not dollar cost.)

05What Makes Melbourne's Coffee World Distinctive

Across these ten views, a pattern emerges. Melbourne's coffee world rests on a narrow, premium-leaning supply chain; a dense, decades-deep concentration of independent venues; an economics so tight that quality is enforced by competition; and a price now rising with global bean shortages. It is less a story of drinking the most coffee than of a city whose whole system — from import to cup — is organised around taking coffee seriously.
